## 4. Introduction

Every project starts somewhere. Before you write a single line of pseudocode, before you draw a flowchart, before you decompose anything — you need to know *what problem you are actually trying to solve*.

This might sound obvious, but it is one of the most common mistakes in computing and software development: jumping straight to building a solution before clearly understanding the problem. You have spent week 1 learning tools for thinking computationally — decomposition, abstraction, algorithms. This final topic of week 1 asks you to pick the area of real life you want to apply those tools to, and write one focused statement about it [1].

That statement is called a **problem statement**. It does not need to be long — in fact, three sentences is exactly the right size at this stage. A short, sharp problem statement forces clarity. It makes you commit to what you are actually tackling before the course carries you forward into specification, design, and building [2].

Your problem statement from this topic will be the seed of your **Specification Portfolio** (Assessment A1, due Week 3). Getting it right now saves you significant rework later.

## 5. Core Concepts

### 5.1 What is a Problem Domain?

A **problem domain** is the specific area of real-world activity that a problem lives in [1].

The word "domain" simply means a field or area. In computing, a problem domain is the slice of the world your solution is meant to address. It is not a technology — it is a real-life context.

Examples of problem domains:

| Domain | Example of a problem within it |
|---|---|
| Healthcare | Patients miss medication doses because they forget |
| Education | Students do not know which revision topics to prioritise |
| Transport | Commuters cannot find a parking space near the train station |
| Retail | Small shops lose track of stock levels manually |
| Environment | Households throw away recyclable items because they are unsure which bin to use |

Your domain is the area you will return to all semester. Choose something you find genuinely interesting or that affects people you know — you will be decomposing it, specifying it, and designing around it for several weeks [2].

### 5.2 What is a Problem Statement?

A **problem statement** is a short, written description that identifies a specific, real problem in your chosen domain, explains who it affects, and states why solving it matters [1].

A good problem statement is:
- **Focused** — it describes one specific problem, not a whole category of problems.
- **Concrete** — it refers to real people, real situations, and real consequences.
- **Bounded** — it does not try to explain the entire domain or propose a solution [3].

A problem statement is *not* a solution proposal. It does not say "I will build an app that…". It says "There is a problem, here is who it affects, and here is why it matters." The solution comes later.

**Example of a vague problem statement (too broad):**
> "Healthcare has a lot of problems. Technology could help make things better for everyone."

This is not useful. It does not identify a specific problem, a specific group of people, or a specific consequence.

**Example of a focused problem statement (well-formed):**
> "Elderly patients living alone often forget to take daily medication at the correct time. This leads to missed doses that can worsen chronic conditions and result in avoidable hospital visits. A simple, automated reminder system could reduce missed doses without requiring the patient to learn new technology."

This is clearer. It names who is affected (elderly patients living alone), what the specific problem is (forgetting medication timing), and what the consequence is (worsened conditions, hospital visits). The third sentence gestures at a potential direction — but that is optional at this stage [2].

### 5.3 The 3-Sentence Format

The most practical way to write your first problem statement is to use a **3-sentence format**. Each sentence does exactly one job [1] [2] [3]:

**Sentence 1 — Context.** Who is affected, and in what situation? Set the scene with a specific group of people or a specific situation, not "everyone" or "society."

**Sentence 2 — The specific problem.** What exactly goes wrong for those people? What is the gap, the failure, or the difficulty? Be precise about the problem itself, not just the general area.

**Sentence 3 — Impact.** Why does this matter? What is the real-world consequence of the problem going unsolved? This can also gesture briefly at what a solution might need to address — but it must not become a solution description.

Laid out as a template:

> [Who / what context] [faces / experiences / encounters] [specific difficulty or gap].
> This [causes / leads to / results in] [specific negative consequence].
> [Addressing / solving] this would require [brief framing of what a solution needs to do — optional].

You do not have to use this exact sentence structure word for word. The point is that each sentence carries one piece of information: context, problem, impact [1].

### 5.4 What Makes a Problem Statement Good?

A well-formed problem statement passes three tests [1] [3]:

1. **Specific, not general.** "People have health problems" is not a problem statement. "Adults over 65 living alone in rural areas often have no way to alert a carer when they fall at home" is a problem statement.

2. **One problem, not a list.** If you find yourself writing "and also…" or "furthermore…", you are describing more than one problem. Pick the most important one and park the others.

3. **No solution embedded in it.** "We need an app to…" or "A website should…" turns a problem statement into a solution pitch. Keep the two separate. The problem statement describes what is broken; the solution design describes how to fix it.

A problem statement that passes these three tests gives you a stable foundation to decompose into sub-problems (using the decomposition skill from topic 1.5) and to abstract away irrelevant detail (using the abstraction skill from topic 1.6) [2].

## 6. Implementation

Writing a problem statement is a procedure. Here is a step-by-step approach:

**Step 1 — Choose your domain.**
Pick an area of real life that interests you. It could be something from your own experience: a frustration you or someone you know encounters regularly, or a gap you have noticed in how something works. Write down the domain in one or two words (e.g., "student revision", "food waste at home", "local transport").

**Step 2 — Identify who is affected.**
Within that domain, who specifically experiences a difficulty? Avoid "everyone." Try to name a particular group: "secondary school students preparing for exams," "restaurant owners with fewer than five staff," "commuters who cycle to work."

**Step 3 — Name the specific problem.**
What exactly goes wrong for that group? Push past the obvious. "Students find revision hard" is too vague. "Students do not know which topics they have already understood versus which ones still need more time" is specific.

**Step 4 — Identify the consequence.**
What happens as a result of the problem going unsolved? "They spend revision time inefficiently, revising topics they already know while neglecting weaker areas, and often only realise this the night before an exam."

**Step 5 — Assemble the three sentences.**
Write Sentence 1 (context), Sentence 2 (specific problem), Sentence 3 (impact). Read it back. Ask: does each sentence do exactly one job? Is the problem specific? Is there any solution language that has crept in?

**Worked example using the student revision domain:**

> Secondary school students preparing for public examinations often have no reliable way to know which topics they understand well and which they are still weak on. As a result, they frequently over-revise familiar material and under-revise the areas that would most improve their grade. A solution to this problem would need to track understanding across topics and surface the weakest areas for a student without requiring a teacher to be present [1] [2].

Note: the third sentence gestures at what a solution needs to do — it does not describe a specific system.

## 7. Real-World Patterns

Problem statements are used at the very start of any serious project — in software teams, research, government policy, and business strategy [1].

In software development, a team begins by writing a problem statement before any technical design work. This prevents the common failure mode of building the wrong thing: spending months developing a solution for a problem no one actually has [2].

In research, a problem statement appears at the start of every academic paper or proposal. It tells the reader exactly what gap in knowledge the work addresses [1].

The 3-sentence format you are learning here is a simplified version of the same discipline used in all these contexts. Full specification writing — expanding a problem statement into detailed requirements — is covered later in the course [3].

## 8. Best Practices

**Do:**
- Use specific, concrete language — name a real group of people and a real situation.
- Keep each sentence to one job: context, problem, impact.
- Read your statement back after writing it and check for solution language that has crept in.
- Choose a domain you genuinely find interesting — you will return to it all semester.

**Do not:**
- Use "everyone" or "society" as the affected group — these are too vague to be useful.
- Combine two different problems into one statement.
- Write a third sentence that proposes a specific solution ("an app that uses AI to…") — keep the solution out of the problem statement.
- Make the domain so broad that it would take a large team years to address.

**The single most common mistake:** writing a problem statement that is actually a solution pitch in disguise. If your statement contains the words "I will build," "the system should," or "using technology to," rewrite it. Describe what is broken, not how to fix it [2] [3].

## 9. Hands-On Exercise

Write a 3-sentence problem statement for a domain you care about. Then apply the three-question test:

1. Is the affected group specific (not "everyone")?
2. Is there exactly one problem described?
3. Is there any solution language? If yes, remove it.

Swap your statement with a partner. Can they answer these three questions from your statement alone, without asking any follow-up questions?

- Who is affected?
- What specifically goes wrong for them?
- What is the consequence?

If they need to ask a clarifying question, that is a signal that one of your three sentences is not doing its job. Revise until the statement stands on its own [1] [2].

## 10. Key Takeaways

- A **problem domain** is the specific area of real-world activity your project addresses — not a technology, but a real-life context.
- A **problem statement** identifies a specific problem, names who it affects, and states why it matters — in three sentences.
- The **3-sentence format** gives each sentence exactly one job: Sentence 1 sets context, Sentence 2 names the specific problem, Sentence 3 states the impact.
- A good problem statement is focused (one problem), concrete (specific people and situations), and solution-free (it describes what is broken, not how to fix it).
- Your problem statement from this topic is the starting point for your Specification Portfolio (A1, due Week 3) — a clear, bounded statement now prevents significant rework later.
