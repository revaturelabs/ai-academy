## 4. Introduction

You have now seen three ways a government can steer AI: a binding law (the EU AI Act, 5.7), voluntary guidance (the NIST framework, 5.10), and a Presidential order (the 2023 executive order, 5.11). India offers a fourth flavor — and it is the softest of the four so far.

In 2024, India's technology ministry, **MEITY** (the **Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology**), issued **advisories** about artificial intelligence [1]. An **advisory** is government guidance — a written note telling companies what the government expects of them — but it is **not** a law passed by a legislature. It is softer than statute, and it can change quickly.

Why teach it? Because India is one of the largest internet markets on earth, and because its approach is a clear, current example of governing AI through *soft guidance* rather than a single hard AI law. It is still **evolving**, but it is heading in the same direction as the frameworks you already know [3]. This topic teaches what the MEITY advisories ask for, why "advisory" matters, and how India lines up with the global picture.

## 5. Core Concepts

### 5.1 Who MEITY is, and what an "advisory" is

**MEITY** stands for the **Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology** — the part of India's central government responsible for technology and the internet [1]. When MEITY wants platforms to behave a certain way, one tool it uses is an advisory.

An **advisory** is written government guidance that tells companies what the government expects them to do [1][2]. Two plain facts make an advisory different from a law:

- **It is guidance, not statute.** A law is passed by a legislature and carries the full force of statute. An advisory is issued by a ministry and is softer — it explains how the government reads the *existing* rules and what it wants platforms to do under them [1][2].
- **It can change fast.** Because no legislature has to vote, a ministry can revise an advisory in days. You will see exactly this happen below [2].

The advisories were aimed mainly at **intermediaries** — online platforms that host or carry other people's content or services, such as social-media sites and search engines [1]. They build on India's existing **IT Rules (2021)**, an earlier set of online-platform rules; you do not need their detail — just note the advisories sit *on top of* rules that were already there [1].

### 5.2 What the advisories actually ask for

Stripped to the essentials, the MEITY AI advisories make two kinds of ask [1][2]:

- **Labeling unreliable or AI-generated output.** Platforms should clearly **label** AI models or outputs that are still **under testing** or **unreliable**, so users know the result may be wrong [1][2]. The same idea applies to AI-made content that could mislead — for example a **deepfake**, a fake image, audio, or video made by AI to look real [1]. Labeling lets a user tell "this was made or judged by an unreliable AI" from genuine human content.
- **Due-diligence duties on platforms.** Platforms must take reasonable care — **due diligence** means making a sincere, documented effort to prevent foreseeable harm — so their AI does not produce unlawful content or amplify bias and misinformation [1][2].

Notice what these two asks are reaching for: **transparency** (label it so it is not hidden) and **harm prevention** (stop the foreseeable damage) — the very pillars from 5.4. India is pursuing familiar goals; the novelty is the *tool*, an advisory rather than a law.

### 5.3 The "evolving" part — one concrete example

The clearest sign that this guidance is still **evolving** is what happened in March 2024 [2]:

- **The March 1, 2024 advisory** asked platforms to get **prior government permission** before deploying AI models that were still **under-tested or unreliable** in India [2]. Many in the industry pushed back, worried this would slow down releasing new AI.
- **The March 15, 2024 revision** — just two weeks later — **dropped the prior-permission requirement** [2]. What it kept was the softer core: **label** under-tested or unreliable models and their output, and exercise due diligence [2].

That two-week turnaround is the whole point in miniature. Because an advisory is guidance, not statute (5.1), the government could revise it almost immediately in response to feedback. A binding law like the EU AI Act (5.7) cannot be re-written that quickly. So when the seed for this topic says India's approach is "evolving," this is the concrete evidence: the rules are still being adjusted, version by version [2].

### 5.4 "Directionally aligned with global frameworks"

The last idea is what India's approach has in *common* with the frameworks you have studied. India is **directionally aligned** with them — meaning it is steering toward the same destination, even if by a different road [3].

- **Same goals.** Transparency about AI, prevention of misinformation and deepfake harm, and care from platforms — these are the same aims behind the EU AI Act (5.7) and the US measures (5.10, 5.11) [3].
- **Different tool, so far.** The EU has one big binding law; the US has used voluntary guidance and an executive order. India, *for now*, is using **advisories** layered on existing online-platform rules, rather than a single dedicated AI law [3].

So "directionally aligned" is an honest, careful phrase: the *direction* matches the global trend, even though the *legal form* is softer and still settling.

### 5.5 Where the MEITY advisories sit beside the others

The cleanest way to hold all four approaches is on one scale, from softest to hardest:

| | NIST AI RMF (5.10) | MEITY advisories (this topic) | EO 14110 (5.11) | EU AI Act (5.7) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| What it is | Voluntary guidance | Government advisory | A US executive order | A binding law |
| Who issues it | NIST (a US agency) | MEITY (an Indian ministry) | The US President | The European Union |
| How binding | Not at all — opt-in | Soft — guidance under existing rules | Commands agencies; some industry reporting | Fully binding by law |
| How fast it changes | Anytime, by choice | Fast — revised in days (Mar 2024) | A later President can revoke it | Slow — it is a law |

The takeaway: the MEITY advisories are **soft guidance** — firmer than purely opt-in advice because they signal what the government expects of platforms, but far softer and more changeable than a binding law [1][2][3].

### 5.6 Context and limits — keeping this honest

Three things keep this topic accurate:

- **These are 2024 advisories, not settled law.** Treat them as evolving soft-law guidance, not a finished statute. India has not, as of this writing, passed a single dedicated AI law [3].
- **It builds on existing rules.** The advisories work *through* India's existing IT Rules (2021), named here only so you know they exist [1].
- **More may follow.** India has discussed broader future technology legislation; such future measures are named here only so you know they exist, and are not covered in this topic.

## 10. Key Takeaways

- **MEITY** is India's **Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology**; in 2024 it issued AI **advisories** — government guidance, not binding law — to online platforms [1].
- The advisories' core asks are to **label** AI-generated, under-tested, or unreliable output (including deepfakes) and to exercise **due diligence** to prevent harm [1][2].
- The guidance is **evolving**: the March 1, 2024 advisory required prior government permission for under-tested models; the March 15, 2024 revision dropped that and kept disclosure and labeling [2].
- India is **directionally aligned with global frameworks** — chasing the same transparency and harm-prevention goals as the EU AI Act (5.7) and US measures (5.10, 5.11), but via advisories rather than a single AI law so far [3].
- On the softness scale, MEITY advisories sit between voluntary guidance (NIST, 5.10) and a binding law (EU AI Act, 5.7) — soft, signal-of-expectation guidance that can be revised quickly [1][2][3].
