## 4. Introduction

In 5.7 you met the EU AI Act's pyramid of risk: four tiers, with heavier rules at higher risk. The very top of that pyramid was the **unacceptable-risk** tier — uses so dangerous that the law does not regulate them, it forbids them.

This topic is that floor of the pyramid up close. Picture a city camera that scans every face in a crowd live and matches it against a watchlist. Picture a government scoring citizens by their everyday behavior and then deciding who gets a loan or a job. These are not science fiction — they are exactly the uses the Act draws a hard line against [1][2].

A **prohibited use** is an AI use the law bans completely. There is no permit, no paperwork, no "high-risk" checklist that makes it legal. It is simply off-limits [1]. These bans live in **Article 5** of the Act — "Article 5" is just the numbered section of the law where the banned list is written down [2].

## 5. Core Concepts

### 5.1 What "prohibited" means (and how it differs from "high-risk")

The Act sorts AI by how much it could harm people. Most tiers say *"you may use this, but follow these rules."* The top tier is different. It says *"you may not use this at all"* [1].

This is the key contrast with the high-risk tier you saw in 5.7 and 5.8:

| Tier | What the law says | What a provider must do |
|---|---|---|
| High-risk | Allowed, but tightly controlled | Documentation, testing, human oversight, then launch |
| Unacceptable (prohibited) | Banned outright | Nothing makes it legal — it simply cannot be deployed |

Why ban instead of regulate? Because for these uses, the harm is judged so severe — a direct attack on human dignity, freedom, or safety — that no amount of paperwork could make them safe [1][3]. This is the **harm-prevention pillar** from 5.4 taken to its limit: when a harm is unacceptable, you prevent it by forbidding it.

### 5.2 Social scoring by governments

**Social scoring** means using AI to rate people based on their behavior or personal traits over time, then treating them well or badly because of that score [1][2].

Here is a concrete example. Imagine a government system that watches what citizens buy, who they spend time with, and whether they pay bills on time. It rolls all of that into a single "trust score." A high score gets you faster loans and better services. A low score gets you turned away from things that have nothing to do with the original behavior [2].

The Act bans this kind of social scoring because it produces two specific harms [2]:

- **Unfair treatment in unrelated situations** — being denied a job because of something you did in a completely different part of your life.
- **Treatment that is out of all proportion** — a small misstep leading to a large, lasting penalty.

Why is this so dangerous? Because it turns your whole life into a permanent grade that follows you everywhere. That strikes directly at fairness and dignity, which is why it sits in the banned tier rather than the regulated one [1].

### 5.3 Real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces

First, two plain definitions:

- **Biometric** — identifying a person from a body trait, such as their face, fingerprint, or the way they walk.
- **Real-time** — happening live, as it occurs. The camera matches faces *in the moment* people walk past, not hours later from a saved recording.

So **real-time remote biometric identification in public spaces** means using AI to scan people live — for example, face-scanning a crowd in a train station and instantly matching faces against a database [1][2].

The Act bans this when it is done by law enforcement in publicly accessible spaces. Live mass face-scanning means everyone in the crowd is identified just for being there. That is a sweeping loss of privacy and freedom for ordinary people who have done nothing wrong [1][2].

### 5.4 The narrow exceptions to the biometric ban

The biometric ban is not absolute. The Act allows a few **narrow, tightly defined exceptions** — and the word *narrow* is the whole point. These are not loopholes. They are short, specific situations, each needing approval in advance [2][3]. The allowed cases are limited to things like:

- Searching for specific victims, such as a missing child or a kidnapping victim.
- Preventing a specific, imminent threat to people's lives, such as a foreseeable terror attack.
- Locating a suspect in a serious crime defined by the law.

Even then, the use must be authorized in advance and limited in time and place [3]. The default is still **banned**. The exceptions are the rare, carefully fenced way out. Understanding the exceptions is how you understand where the boundary of the ban actually sits — a system that scans crowds live for general policing is on the banned side of that line [1][3].

### 5.5 The other prohibited practices (at a beginner's level)

Social scoring and real-time biometric surveillance are the two headline bans, but Article 5 lists several more. You only need to recognize them, not memorize legal detail [1][2]:

- **Manipulation that causes harm** — AI that uses hidden or deceptive techniques to push people into decisions they would not otherwise make, in a way that hurts them.
- **Exploiting vulnerabilities** — AI that takes advantage of someone's age, disability, or difficult situation to distort their behavior.
- **Emotion recognition at work or school** — AI that claims to read people's emotions in workplaces or classrooms (with limited safety and medical exceptions).
- **Untargeted face-scraping** — building face databases by mass-collecting images from the internet or street cameras.
- **Predictive policing of individuals** — AI that predicts a person will commit a crime based purely on profiling them, not on real evidence.
- **Biometric categorization of sensitive traits** — sorting people by sensitive characteristics such as race, political views, or beliefs using their body data.

The common thread across the whole list: each is a use where the harm to people's rights is judged too great to allow under any conditions [1][2].

### 5.6 How this connects back to the pyramid and the pillars

This topic is simply the top block of the 5.7 pyramid, examined closely:

- The **unacceptable-risk tier** equals these prohibited uses. Few in number, sitting at the narrow top.
- The reason they are banned, not regulated, is the **harm-prevention pillar** from 5.4 — some harms are stopped by forbidding the practice entirely.
- **Fairness and accountability** (also from 5.4) explain the social-scoring ban: no one should be graded and penalized across their whole life by an opaque score.

So nothing here is brand-new ethics. It is the four pillars and the risk pyramid you already know, applied to the worst cases [1][2].

## 8. Best Practices

For a beginner, the goal is to *recognize a prohibited use when you see one*, not to apply the law. Useful heuristics:

- **Ask "does this judge or watch people for who they are?"** Social scoring judges people; live biometric surveillance watches them. Both are red flags for the banned tier.
- **"Banned" is rare and deliberate.** Most AI is minimal-risk (5.7). Only a short, specific list is prohibited — do not assume every worrying AI is banned.
- **Watch the word "narrow."** The biometric exceptions are tiny and pre-authorized. If someone describes broad, everyday live face-scanning as "an exception," that is a red flag, not a real exception.
- **Trace it back to harm.** If a use attacks dignity, freedom, or fairness so directly that no checklist could fix it, that is why it sits in the banned tier, not the high-risk one.

## 10. Key Takeaways

- A **prohibited use** is an AI use the EU AI Act bans completely — it is the **unacceptable-risk** tier from 5.7, and it lives in **Article 5** [1][2].
- **Social scoring by governments** — rating people by behavior and then treating them unfairly in unrelated situations — is banned because it attacks fairness and dignity [2].
- **Real-time biometric surveillance** — live face-scanning of people in public spaces — is banned, with only **narrow, pre-authorized exceptions** like finding a missing child or stopping an imminent attack [1][3].
- Article 5 also bans harmful **manipulation**, **exploiting vulnerabilities**, **emotion recognition at work/school**, untargeted **face-scraping**, individual **predictive policing**, and sensitive **biometric categorization** [1][2].
- These uses are **banned rather than regulated** because the harm is too severe for any safeguard to fix — the **harm-prevention pillar** from 5.4 at its limit [1][3].
