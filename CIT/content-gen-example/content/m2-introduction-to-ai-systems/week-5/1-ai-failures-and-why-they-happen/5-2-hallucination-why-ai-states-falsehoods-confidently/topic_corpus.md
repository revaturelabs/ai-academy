## 4. Introduction

You search for a legal case. A lawyer uses an AI assistant to help prepare court documents. The AI returns a list of past judgments — complete with case names, court dates, and case numbers. They look real. They are written exactly like real legal citations. The lawyer submits them to the court.

The cases do not exist. Every single one was invented by the AI.

In 2023, this happened to a real lawyer in a real U.S. federal court. The judge fined him. The profession was embarrassed. And the AI had shown no hesitation, no warning, no "I'm not sure about this." It presented the made-up citations with the same confident tone it uses for facts that are completely correct [1].

This is the problem topic 5.2 is about. Not just that AI gets things wrong — you already know that from topic 3.9 and topic 5.1. The specific question here is: **why does AI get things wrong so confidently?** Why doesn't it say "I don't know"?

The answer lies in how large language models are built. Understanding it will change the way you read any AI output for the rest of your career.

## 5. Core Concepts

### 5.1 Quick orientation — LLMs and tokens

To understand why hallucination happens, you need a clear picture of how an LLM works. Here is the essential minimum.

**LLM (Large Language Model)** — a type of AI system trained on enormous amounts of text. It learns patterns in that text — which words tend to follow which other words, in which contexts. After training, it can generate new text that follows those patterns.

**Token** — a small chunk of text that an LLM reads and writes, roughly the size of a word or part of a word. The model processes text as a stream of tokens, one at a time.

**Training** — the process of showing an LLM a huge collection of text and adjusting the model's internal settings so that its predictions match the real text as closely as possible.

**Inference** — what happens when you actually use an LLM. You send it a prompt; it generates a response. This is the live, real-time process of the model producing output.

These concepts were introduced earlier in the M2 sequence. They are included here as the foundation for the mechanism explained below.

### 5.2 Next-token prediction — the engine behind everything

Every output an LLM produces is generated by one repeated action: **next-token prediction**.

**Next-token prediction** — the process by which an LLM looks at all the tokens it has seen so far (your question, plus anything it has written so far in the reply) and picks the most likely next token to add.

Here is the step-by-step version of how the model writes a single response:

1. You type a question — for example, "Who wrote the play Hamlet?"
2. The LLM looks at those tokens. Based on everything it learned during training, it asks: "Given this context, what is the most likely next token?"
3. It picks a token — say, "William."
4. Now it has a new context: your question plus "William." It asks again: what comes next?
5. It picks "Shakespeare."
6. It continues — period, end — until it decides the response is complete.

Every single token is produced by this same calculation. There is no separate step where the model checks a database of facts. There is no step where it asks itself "am I sure this is true?" It is always, only, asking: "What text is most likely to come next?" [1]

This is the engine that produces both the model's correct answers and its hallucinations. The mechanism is identical in both cases. The difference is whether the patterns the model learned happened to correspond to something real in the world.

### 5.3 Why the model has no built-in uncertainty signal

When a human is unsure of something, they can feel it. They might say "I think" or "I'm not certain about that." They have access to their own confidence level as a felt experience.

An LLM has no equivalent internal state [1].

The model produces a probability distribution over possible next tokens — a mathematical ranking of how likely each possible word is. But those probabilities are not visible to you as the user. You receive only the words the model chose. And those words are chosen to be the most fluent, coherent continuation of the prompt — not the most cautious or qualified one [2].

There is a concept called **calibration** — whether a model's internal confidence scores actually reflect how often it is correct. A well-calibrated model would assign high confidence to answers it usually gets right, and low confidence to answers it often gets wrong. Current LLMs are frequently miscalibrated: they assign high probability to tokens that produce false statements [2].

Put simply: the model's internal maths can be very confident about a wrong answer. And that internal confidence shows up on your screen as fluent, authoritative-sounding prose.

### 5.4 Two types of hallucination

Researchers distinguish two types of hallucination. Understanding the difference helps you identify what went wrong in any specific case [2][3].

**Intrinsic hallucination** — when the AI produces output that directly contradicts information that was provided to it in the prompt or in its training data. The model had the correct information; it just did not use it faithfully.

- Example: You ask an AI to summarize a document you pasted in. The summary says the report recommends Option A. The document actually recommends Option B. The correct answer was right there in the text you provided. The model garbled it.

**Extrinsic hallucination** — when the AI produces output that cannot be verified against any source — content that was simply invented. The fabricated legal citations in the Introduction are a classic example. The model produced text that looked like real legal references, but no source in its training data supported those specific citations [3].

Both types produce the same surface experience: fluent, confident, plausible-looking text. The difference is in the root cause.

| Type | What happened | Key signal |
|---|---|---|
| Intrinsic | Model contradicted source material it had available | Check the source you provided |
| Extrinsic | Model invented content with no supporting source | The content cannot be verified anywhere |

### 5.5 Why the model cannot just say "I don't know"

This is the question that surprises most beginners. If the model isn't sure, why doesn't it just say so?

The short answer: the model was not originally built to track what it knows versus what it doesn't. It was built to produce fluent text that continues a prompt. "I don't know" is just another possible sequence of tokens. Whether the model outputs those tokens depends on whether its training data included examples of saying "I don't know" in contexts that match your prompt — not on whether the model has a genuine assessment of its own knowledge [1].

There is a deeper issue. The model learned from text written by humans. Humans mostly write text that asserts things — explains things, states facts, tells stories. The web contains far more confident assertions than admissions of uncertainty. So the model learned a style that matches that distribution: confident, assertive, helpful-sounding prose [2].

This is why the problem is called hallucination rather than simply "errors." The model does not know it is wrong. It is not trying to deceive you. It is doing exactly what it was trained to do — produce the most likely next token — and that process generates false statements with the same fluency it uses for true ones.

### 5.6 The confidence problem — why this is worse than a simple mistake

If AI produced false answers with clear hedging — "I believe this but am not certain" — users could calibrate. They would know to check. The specific danger of hallucination is that the false answer looks exactly like a correct one [1].

Consider two possible AI outputs:

- **Output A:** "I think the author may have been born around 1820, but you should verify this."
- **Output B:** "The author was born on 14 March 1820 in Edinburgh."

Output B looks more authoritative. Most users trust it more. If both are hallucinations, Output B causes more harm — because a user is far more likely to act on it without checking.

LLMs tend to produce Output B. Their training on confident human prose pushes them toward specificity and certainty. Dates, names, case numbers, and statistics appear frequently in LLM output because these are common in human text — but they are also the elements most likely to be fabricated precisely when the model has no real grounding for them [2].

This is the "confidently" in the topic title. The confidence is not deception. It is the natural output of a system optimized for fluent, plausible text rather than verified, true text.

### 5.7 What makes some domains more dangerous than others

Hallucination happens across all types of LLM use. But not all hallucinations cause the same level of harm. The stakes depend on what the false output is used for [1][2].

**Low-stakes context:** An AI helps you brainstorm titles for a blog post. If a suggested title rests on a misunderstood premise, you notice easily and discard it. The harm is minimal.

**High-stakes context:** An AI generates a clinical summary for a patient's medical chart. A hallucinated drug dosage or contraindication could cause serious injury. The output looks exactly like a correct medical note. A busy clinician may not verify every detail.

The domains where hallucination is most dangerous are those you met in topic 5.1:

- **Healthcare** — false clinical information, especially when the AI's output is trusted without verification.
- **Legal** — fabricated citations, incorrect case law, wrong statutes.
- **Finance** — hallucinated figures, invented contract terms, wrong regulatory rules.
- **News and information** — false statements that look like verified facts, spread at scale.

In every high-stakes domain, the pattern is the same: the AI produces a confident wrong answer, the user trusts the confidence, and the error causes real harm before anyone checks [1].

### 5.8 Named mitigations — to be covered later

Researchers and engineers have developed approaches to reduce hallucination. These include RAG, grounding (anchoring AI output to verified sources), and RLHF. Each is named here only so you know that solutions exist — none of them is taught in this topic. You will cover them in later topics.

## 6. Implementation

This topic does not involve a technical procedure. But it does introduce a structured critical-reading habit you can apply immediately. When you receive any AI-generated response, step through this in order:

1. **Identify the high-stakes claims.** Names, dates, figures, citations, legal references, medical facts — these token types are most frequently hallucinated because the model generates them fluently from pattern alone.
2. **Check against a primary source.** For any high-stakes claim, locate the source independently. Do not use another AI to verify an AI — you are just adding another hallucination risk.
3. **Look for precision that seems too convenient.** Exact dates, exact case numbers, precise statistics — flag these if you cannot independently verify them. Hallucinated content often has a suspicious specificity.
4. **Notice whether the model hedged at all.** If the model produced no uncertainty signals in a domain where uncertainty is normal, treat the entire response with extra caution.
5. **Ask yourself: does this domain have high harm stakes?** If yes, raise your verification standard. An error in a casual email draft is annoying. An error in a medical, legal, or financial document is dangerous.

## 7. Real-World Patterns

**The fake legal citations case (2023).** A U.S. lawyer submitted court documents containing case citations generated by an AI assistant. Every cited case was a hallucination — fabricated names, case numbers, and courts that did not exist. The judge discovered the fraud during review and fined the lawyer. This case became one of the most widely reported demonstrations that AI hallucination causes real-world professional harm [1].

**AI hallucination in medical summaries.** Multiple healthcare studies have found that AI-generated clinical summaries contain factual errors — including hallucinated drug dosages, contraindications, and patient history details — at rates significant enough to concern regulators. These are exactly the categories where a single error can cause direct patient harm [1][2].

**News organizations and AI-generated errors.** Several major news organizations that experimented with AI-written articles in 2022–2024 discovered significant factual errors — hallucinated quotes, wrong dates, and invented statistics. Several retracted AI-assisted articles as a result [2].

**Enterprise deployments.** Companies deploying LLMs for customer-facing responses have documented cases where the AI invented return policies, contract terms, and product specifications — creating legal and reputational exposure [1].

## 8. Best Practices

| Practice | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Treat every AI output as a draft, not a final answer | Fluency is not evidence of accuracy |
| Verify high-stakes claims from a primary source — not another AI | Independent verification is the only reliable check |
| Flag suspicious precision — exact citations, exact numbers — for verification | Hallucinated content is frequently and specifically detailed because specificity matches confident human prose |
| Tell your team the AI was used and that claims need checking | Organizational accountability requires transparency about AI use |
| In high-stakes domains, require human expert review before acting | AI confidence is not calibrated to correctness |

**Anti-pattern to avoid:** Assuming that a well-known or highly-rated AI tool does not hallucinate. All current LLMs hallucinate. The frequency varies; the risk is present in every system. Reputation is not verification.

## 10. Key Takeaways

- LLMs produce every word by **next-token prediction** — picking the most statistically likely next token given the context. This process has no built-in truth-checking step. Correct outputs and hallucinated outputs are generated by exactly the same mechanism.
- The model has no internal uncertainty signal it can share with you. The fluent, confident prose it produces is an artifact of training on confident human text — not a sign that the content is accurate.
- **Intrinsic hallucination** contradicts information the model was given. **Extrinsic hallucination** invents content with no source. Both appear as confident, fluent text — indistinguishable on the surface.
- The harm from hallucination scales with the stakes of the domain. A hallucinated blog title is harmless. A hallucinated drug dosage or legal citation can cause serious real-world harm.
- The most dangerous aspect of hallucination is not the error itself — it is the confidence that wraps it. Users trust confident outputs. That trust, when misplaced, is what allows hallucination to cause harm at scale.
