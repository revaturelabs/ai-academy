## 4. Introduction

You have designed your rubric, assigned weights, run your test protocol, and scored two AI tools. You have a table of numbers in front of you. Now comes the part that actually matters: turning those numbers into a recommendation that another person can trust.

Here is the challenge most people run into. When someone asks "which tool is better?", the natural answer is how you feel: "I liked Tool A — it felt smarter." That is an honest reaction. But it is not a recommendation. Another person cannot check it. Your lecturer cannot grade it. Your team cannot act on it.

An **evidence-based recommendation** is different. It says: "Based on our five-criteria rubric, Tool A scored 3.2 out of 4 and Tool B scored 2.6 out of 4. Tool A's lead came from higher scores on functionality (4 out of 4, versus 2 out of 4 for Tool B) and accuracy (3 out of 4, versus 2 out of 4). We recommend Tool A — specifically because it completed all five test tasks without error, while Tool B failed two." Every claim in that statement points to something you can show [1].

Think of a food-safety inspector. The inspector does not say "this restaurant smells fine to me." They record specific scores — food temperature, surface cleanliness, pest control — and the recommendation to pass or close follows from those scores. Your rubric is that scoring framework. You already ran the inspection. Now you write the report.

This skill feeds directly into your A2 AI Systems Essay (due week 6), where you will present a structured, evidence-backed comparison of AI tools in writing [2].

## 5. Core Concepts

### 5.1 Evidence vs Opinion — the Core Distinction

**Opinion** — a personal preference or feeling that cannot be checked by another person.
> Example: "Tool A seemed more intelligent."

**Evidence** — a specific, recorded result tied to your rubric, test protocol, or documented observation. Another person with your rubric and the same test could check it.
> Example: "Tool A scored 4 out of 5 on the accuracy criterion in three test runs. Tool B scored 2 out of 5 on the same runs." [1]

The practical difference matters. A recommendation built on opinion can be dismissed with a counter-opinion — "Well, I liked Tool B better." A recommendation built on evidence can only be challenged by questioning the rubric design or the test protocol — both of which you have on record and can defend [1][2].

A quick test you can apply to any sentence before writing it:

| Statement | Can another person check it? | Type |
|---|---|---|
| "Tool A felt more natural to use." | No — it is a feeling | Opinion |
| "Tool A scored 4/5 on ease-of-use in all three test runs." | Yes — the score sheet is on record | Evidence |
| "I think Tool B is better for our team." | No — it is a belief | Opinion |
| "Tool B scored highest on the collaboration criterion (weight: 20%)." | Yes — rubric and scores are on record | Evidence |

Every sentence in a findings-based recommendation should pass the "can another person check this?" test [1].

### 5.2 The Three Parts of a Well-Formed Recommendation

A strong recommendation has three parts, presented in this order [1][2][3]:

**Part 1 — Findings summary.**
One or two sentences that state what the evaluation found. Name both tools, reference the rubric, and give the overall weighted totals.

> Example: "Using a five-criteria rubric weighted toward accuracy and accessibility, Tool A scored 3.2 out of 4 and Tool B scored 2.6 out of 4 across ten test tasks."

**Part 2 — Evidence anchor.**
Two or three specific results — criterion name, score for each tool, and a concrete observation from the test — that most strongly explain why the totals landed where they did.

> Example: "Tool A's lead came from functionality (4/4 versus 2/4) and accuracy (3/4 versus 2/4). In three of the five test tasks, Tool B produced outputs that contained factual errors; Tool A's outputs on the same tasks required no correction."

**Part 3 — Recommendation statement.**
A clear conclusion, tied directly to the findings. Include any important conditions or limitations.

> Example: "We recommend Tool A for student coursework support. If this tool will be used with sensitive personal data, the privacy criterion — where both tools scored equally (3/4) — should be re-evaluated with a stricter test protocol before a final decision."

These three parts work together. A recommendation without a findings summary looks arbitrary. A findings summary without an evidence anchor is just a number. An evidence anchor without a clear recommendation statement leaves the reader unsure what to do [1][2].

### 5.3 Translating Rubric Scores into Cited Evidence

Rubric scores are your evidence — but scores alone are too thin. A score of 2/4 on accuracy tells someone the tool underperformed, but does not show what you actually saw. The evidence anchor must do two things: name the descriptor level that matched your observation, and point to a specific example from your test [2][3].

**A usable format for citing rubric evidence:**

1. State the tool name and the criterion score.
2. Identify the descriptor level that matched what you observed.
3. Give a specific example from the test that shows why that descriptor applies.

> Example: "Tool B scored 2/4 on accuracy. This matches descriptor level 2 ('Several factual errors are present; a user relying on the output without checking would be misled on at least one point'). In Task 3, Tool B stated that a named AI system was released in 2022; the confirmed release date is 2023."

You do not need to do this for every criterion. Focus on the criteria that drove the conclusion. If Tool A won primarily on functionality and accuracy, those two criteria deserve full evidence citations. Other criteria can be summarised briefly or referenced in the findings summary table [1].

**Building a findings summary table.** Before writing the recommendation in prose, collect all your scores in one place:

| Criterion | Weight | Tool A score | Tool B score | Difference |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Functionality | 30% | 4/4 | 2/4 | +2 Tool A |
| Accuracy | 25% | 3/4 | 2/4 | +1 Tool A |
| Privacy | 20% | 3/4 | 3/4 | Tie |
| Accessibility | 15% | 3/4 | 4/4 | +1 Tool B |
| Ethics and Bias | 10% | 3/4 | 3/4 | Tie |
| **Weighted total** | 100% | **3.20/4** | **2.60/4** | **+0.60 Tool A** |

The table does not make the decision — you do. But it makes the decision transparent. Anyone reading your recommendation can verify the totals and trace each number back to the rubric [2].

### 5.4 Acknowledging Trade-offs

A strong recommendation names the cases where the recommended tool scored lower than the other. Ignoring trade-offs makes your recommendation look like advocacy, not analysis [2][3].

**A trade-off** is a criterion where the tool you are not recommending actually outperformed the one you are recommending.

> Example: "Tool B scored 4/4 on accessibility, compared to Tool A's 3/4. For contexts where the tool will be used by learners with disabilities or on low-bandwidth connections, this difference is significant and should be factored back into the rubric before a final decision."

Naming the trade-off is not weakness. It shows you looked at the full table, not just the total. Readers who notice the lower score will trust your recommendation far more if you address it directly [1][2].

### 5.5 Stating Limitations

**Limitations** are gaps in your test design that might affect the conclusion. Stating them briefly is standard practice in evidence-based reasoning [2][3].

Common limitations to acknowledge:

- **Test sample size:** "Five test tasks is a starter evaluation. A full organisational review would use a larger and more varied set."
- **Single scorer:** "This rubric was scored by one person. A multi-scorer approach would test whether different evaluators reach the same conclusion."
- **Version and timing:** "Both tools were tested in June 2026. Both are updated regularly; scores may change with new versions."
- **Context specificity:** "The weights reflect a student audience. A professional legal-drafting context would require different weights."

Limitations bound your recommendation — they do not undermine it. A recommendation stated without limitations claims more certainty than a five-task rubric can actually deliver [3].

### 5.6 Three Common Weaknesses to Avoid

These patterns appear regularly in first drafts. Learn to spot them in your own writing [1][2]:

**Weakness 1 — Vague claims without evidence**

The word "better" alone is vague.

| Weak | Stronger |
|---|---|
| "Tool A produced better outputs overall." | "Tool A's outputs required no editing in 4 of 5 tasks; Tool B required editing in all 5." |
| "Tool B felt harder to use." | "Tool B scored 2/4 on accessibility; completion of the first task took 8 minutes versus 2 minutes on Tool A." |

**Weakness 2 — Unsupported assertions**

An unsupported assertion claims something that is not backed by any rubric score or observation.

| Weak | Stronger |
|---|---|
| "Tool B is more ethical." | "Tool B scored 4/4 on Ethics and Bias — no output used exclusionary language in any of the five tasks, matching descriptor level 4. Tool A scored 3/4; one output in Task 2 used a phrasing flagged by descriptor level 3 as 'potentially exclusionary in some contexts.'" |

**Weakness 3 — Ignored trade-offs**

If the recommended tool scored lower on a criterion, naming it and explaining why it did not change the conclusion is essential.

| Weak | Stronger |
|---|---|
| "Therefore we recommend Tool A." (when Tool A scored lower on accessibility) | "We recommend Tool A. Tool B outperformed on accessibility (4/4 versus 3/4). If this tool will be used by a population with accessibility needs, that criterion should be reweighted before a final decision." |

## 6. Implementation

### Writing Your Recommendation — Step by Step

Use this procedure after completing the rubric-scoring phase from topic 4.7.

1. **Check your rubric table is complete.** Every criterion must have a score for both tools. Weighted totals must be calculated and verified.

2. **Build a findings summary table** using the five-column format from section 5.3. This table is your reference for everything else.

3. **Identify the deciding criteria.** Look for the criteria with the largest score gaps between the two tools, especially on the highest-weighted criteria. These are the criteria you will feature in your evidence anchor.

4. **Write Part 1 — Findings summary.** One or two sentences. Name both tools, name the rubric (or describe it briefly), and state the overall weighted totals.

5. **Write Part 2 — Evidence anchor.** For each of the two or three deciding criteria: state the criterion name, the scores for both tools, the descriptor level that matched what you observed, and one specific test observation. Aim for two to four sentences per criterion.

6. **Identify trade-offs.** For each criterion where the lower-scoring tool actually won, write one sentence: name the criterion, state the scores, and say why it does (or does not) change the recommendation in your context.

7. **Write Part 3 — Recommendation statement.** State your recommendation directly and clearly. Name any trade-off that limits or conditions it. Name any context under which the recommendation would need to be revisited.

8. **Add a limitations note.** Two to four sentences: how many test tasks you ran, whether you scored alone, when you ran the test, and any other relevant constraints.

9. **Read it aloud and remove opinion language.** Listen for: "felt," "seemed," "I think," "probably," "better" (used alone). Each instance is an opportunity to replace it with a rubric reference.

## 7. Real-World Patterns

### How Professionals Use the Same Structure

The three-part structure — findings summary, evidence anchor, recommendation statement — appears across many professional contexts where a comparison leads to a decision [3].

**Technology procurement.** A university committee evaluating two AI writing tools for student use produces a recommendation document before any purchase. The committee applies a rubric covering privacy, accessibility, accuracy, and academic-integrity risk. Each evaluator scores independently; then the group compares scores and resolves disagreements. The final recommendation follows the same pattern taught here: overall scores, deciding criteria, recommendation with conditions [1][3].

**Research summaries for non-technical audiences.** When a technical team presents findings to a leadership group, the recommendation must be legible to people who have not seen the raw data. The evidence anchor is what does this work — it selects the two or three most important results and explains why they matter, without requiring the reader to interpret an entire scoring table [2][3].

**AI tool adoption decisions.** As AI tools multiply in 2026, organisations need documented, auditable justifications for the tools they adopt. A structured recommendation grounded in rubric scores gives decision-makers something they can review, challenge, and revisit later if conditions change [1][2].

## 8. Best Practices

**Do:**

- Build the findings summary table before writing a single sentence of prose. The table is the anchor for everything else.
- Name the criterion, the score, and the descriptor level in every evidence statement. All three together make evidence citable.
- Address trade-offs directly. The reader who noticed that the recommended tool scored lower on one criterion will trust you far more if you acknowledge it.
- State limitations before submitting. Two or three sentences are enough. Limitations bound the recommendation — they do not retract it.
- State the context of the recommendation. "For a student audience" or "for customer query handling" — this reminds the reader that your recommendation is conditional on the use case, not universal.

**Do not:**

- Adjust weights after scoring to favour the tool you already prefer. That is fabrication, not evaluation.
- Use opinion language — "felt," "seemed," "I believe" — anywhere in the recommendation. Replace each instance with a rubric reference.
- Present the overall total without identifying which criteria drove it. A total score without a breakdown gives the reader no way to trace your conclusion.
- Recommend both tools simultaneously. "Both have their strengths" is a deferral, not a recommendation. If two tools genuinely suit different use cases, write two separate recommendations.
- Over-claim. "This tool is definitively better" is stronger than your rubric can support. "Based on this evaluation, we recommend Tool A for this context" is honest and defensible.

## 10. Key Takeaways

- An **evidence-based recommendation** ties every claim to a rubric score, a descriptor match, or a recorded test observation. Opinion language ("felt," "seemed," "probably") is replaced by traceable evidence.
- A well-formed recommendation has three parts in order: **findings summary** (overall scores from the rubric), **evidence anchor** (the two or three criteria that drove the result, with specific observations), and **recommendation statement** (a clear conclusion with any conditions or limitations).
- **Trade-offs** — criteria where the recommended tool scored lower — must be named, not ignored. Acknowledging them shows honest analysis and makes the recommendation more trustworthy, not less.
- **Limitations** — gaps in test scope, single-evaluator scoring, specific version and timing — should be stated briefly. They define the boundary of the recommendation; they do not invalidate it.
- The same three-part structure applies to the A2 AI Systems Essay: findings, evidence, recommendation. Evidence without a conclusion is incomplete; a conclusion without evidence is an opinion.
