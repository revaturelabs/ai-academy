# 1. The Judgment Framework — Q2: Can I verify this without the AI? — Topic Corpus

## 3. Learning Objectives

By the end of this topic you will be able to:

2. Distinguish between verification (checking an AI claim against another source) and production (generating the same output yourself from scratch).
3. Identify at least three methods of verification — cross-referencing a source, applying domain expertise, checking against a known standard, and asking a second person.

## 4. Introduction

You just asked an AI system a question and it gave you an answer. The answer looks right. It is clearly written and sounds confident. Should you act on it?

Q1 of the Judgment Framework told you how much it matters if the answer is wrong. Q2 asks something different: can you actually check whether the answer is correct — without relying on the AI to check itself? [1]

This is harder than it sounds. The AI that produced the answer cannot tell you whether the answer is trustworthy — it has no independent view of its own output. Asking the same AI "are you sure?" does not give you new information. It gives you the same model's self-assessment, which is shaped by the same training data and the same patterns that produced the original answer.

What you need is a way to verify the claim using a source that did not come from the AI. This topic explains what that means, when it is possible, when it is difficult, and what to do when it is genuinely not possible. [2]

## 5. Core Concepts

### 5.1 What "independent verification" means

**Independent verification** is the act of checking an AI output against a source that is completely separate from the AI that produced it. The key word is "independent." The checking source must not be the same AI, a closely related AI, or output that originally came from an AI and was recycled into your reference material.

Think of it like this: if a friend tells you a rumour and you want to know if it is true, asking that same friend again does not help. You need a second source — someone who was not part of the original conversation and who has their own knowledge of the situation. [3]

The same logic applies to AI output. A second independent source might be:
- A textbook, official publication, or peer-reviewed article.
- A qualified expert (a doctor, lawyer, engineer, or other professional) who can evaluate the claim from their own knowledge.
- A known standard or regulation you can look up directly.
- A measurement, test result, or direct observation you can perform yourself.
- A second human colleague who can review the claim without first reading the AI's answer.

### 5.2 Verification is not the same as production

Students often confuse two different questions:

- "Could I have produced this output myself?" — This is the **production question**. It asks whether you have enough expertise to have written or calculated the same thing from scratch.
- "Can I check whether this output is correct?" — This is the **verification question**. It asks whether you can test or confirm the claim using a separate source.

These are not the same thing. [1]

You might be able to verify without being able to produce. A patient who does not know how to diagnose a disease can still check that the dosage on a prescription matches the standard reference range printed in a drug reference guide. They are verifying, not producing.

You might also be able to produce without being sure you can verify. A junior developer who knows the language well enough to write the same code might still not know which of two different implementations is faster without running a benchmark test.

The distinction matters because Q2 is always about verification, not production. Do not answer "no" to Q2 simply because you could not have produced the output yourself. [2]

### 5.3 The four main verification methods

**Cross-referencing a source** means taking a specific claim from the AI output and looking it up in a separate, authoritative reference. For example: the AI says a particular drug interaction is dangerous. You check that claim in a pharmacology database or a prescribing guide that was written by medical professionals and is updated independently of any AI. [1]

**Using domain expertise** means drawing on your own professional or subject knowledge to evaluate whether the AI's output is plausible, accurate, and complete. For example: a structural engineer reads an AI-generated load calculation and recognises that the assumed safety factor is too low by their professional standard. Their own training and experience is the independent source. This method only works when the person checking has genuine expertise in the relevant domain. [2]

**Checking against a known standard** means comparing the AI output to an established rule, law, regulation, formula, or specification. For example: the AI generates a contract clause and you compare it line by line to the wording required by the relevant legislation. The legislation is your independent standard. This method is powerful when a clear written standard exists — it removes subjectivity from the check. [1]

**Asking a second person** means having another human review the AI output before you act on it. The second person should ideally not have seen the AI's answer first, so their assessment is truly independent. If they read the AI output before forming their own view, they may be anchored (a bias you studied in 9.4) to the AI's framing. [3]

### 5.4 When verification is straightforward

Verification is straightforward when:
- The AI's claim is factual and specific (a date, a number, a named standard).
- A reliable reference exists that covers exactly that fact.
- You can access that reference quickly.
- The claim is in an area where you or a colleague have enough background to know what a correct answer looks like.

Example: An AI says the EU General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) requires breach notification within 72 hours. You verify this in under two minutes by reading Article 33 of the GDPR directly. The claim is factual, the standard is written down, and the document is publicly available. [1]

### 5.5 When verification is difficult

Verification is difficult when:
- The claim is about something specialised that requires expert training to evaluate.
- No single authoritative reference exists — the "right answer" is contested or context-dependent.
- The verification would take significantly longer than the task itself, creating a practical barrier.
- The relevant expert is not available in time.

Example: An AI summarises the findings of a technical research paper. To verify the summary fully, you would need to read the original paper in full and have expertise in the methods used. This is possible in principle but takes significant time and expertise. [2]

Difficult verification does not mean you skip it. It means you plan for it — allow more time, identify who has the relevant expertise, or escalate the check.

### 5.6 When verification is genuinely impossible

Verification is sometimes genuinely impossible:
- The AI has generated a creative or predictive output that has no external ground truth. (What will the stock price be in six months? There is no reference to check against.)
- The domain knowledge required does not exist anywhere yet — the AI is working at the frontier of knowledge.
- The timeline is so short that no meaningful check can be done before action is required.
- You have no access to any independent source.

When verification is genuinely impossible, Q2 has a clear answer: "No, I cannot verify this." The Judgment Framework then pushes you to Q1: if you already know from Q1 that the cost of being wrong is high, and Q2 tells you that you cannot verify the answer, the correct action is to not act on the AI output alone. You need a different source, a different approach, or an explicit decision by an accountable person. [1][3]

### 5.7 The interaction between Q1 and Q2

Q1 and Q2 work together. The combination of your answers determines what action is appropriate.

| Q1 answer (cost of being wrong) | Q2 answer (can I verify?) | Recommended action |
|---|---|---|
| Low | Yes | Act, then verify when convenient. |
| Low | No | Act with awareness that error is undetected, review later. |
| High | Yes | Verify before acting. Do not act until verified. |
| High | No | Do not act on AI output alone. Escalate, seek expert input, or use a different approach. |

The bottom-right cell is the most important. When cost is high and verification is impossible, acting on the AI output without further steps is a failure of judgment — regardless of how confident the AI seems. [1][2]

## 6. Implementation (Step-by-Step Verification Procedure)

When you receive an AI output and need to apply Q2, follow these steps.

**Step 1 — List the specific claims.** Break the AI output into individual claims or facts. Do not try to verify a long paragraph as a whole. Verify claim by claim.

**Step 2 — Classify each claim.** For each claim, ask: what kind of thing is this — a factual statement, a calculation, a legal interpretation, a prediction, or a recommendation? The type of claim tells you what kind of verification method is appropriate.

**Step 3 — Identify a verification source.** For each claim, name a specific independent source you will use. "I'll check it online" is not a source. "I will check this in the British National Formulary (drug dosage guide)" or "I will ask our senior engineer" is a source.

**Step 4 — Perform the check.** Look up the claim in your identified source. Record what the source says. If the AI's claim matches, verification passes. If it differs, the AI is wrong on that claim.

**Step 5 — Record your Q2 answer.** State clearly: "I can / cannot verify this" and note the method or reason. This record is the evidence trail that an accountable person needs if the decision is later questioned. [1][3]

## 7. Real-World Patterns

### Legal: AI-generated contract clauses

A law firm uses an AI tool to draft standard contract clauses faster. Before any AI-generated clause goes into a final contract, a qualified lawyer checks each clause against the relevant legislation and the firm's own precedent library. The check is done clause by clause. The lawyer is not re-drafting from scratch (they could, but that defeats the efficiency gain). They are verifying — comparing what the AI produced against an authoritative independent standard. If the AI clause deviates from the statutory requirement, it is corrected before the contract goes out. This is a professional verification workflow applied to a high-cost, high-stakes domain. [1]

### Medical: AI-suggested drug dosage

A hospital deploys an AI clinical decision support tool. When the tool recommends a drug dosage, the prescribing doctor checks the recommendation against the hospital's formulary (an approved list of drugs and doses maintained by clinical pharmacists). The doctor also applies their own clinical judgment about the patient's weight, kidney function, and other medications. Two independent checks are in play: the formulary and expert clinical reasoning. The AI recommendation is a starting point, not the final word. This reflects the principle that in high-stakes medical decisions, verification is mandatory, not optional. [2]

### Financial: AI-generated market analysis

A financial analyst uses an AI tool to summarise quarterly earnings data. Before sharing the summary with clients, the analyst cross-references the key figures against the company's actual earnings release filed with the stock exchange regulator. Numbers that appear in public regulatory filings are independently verifiable. The AI might have made an arithmetic error or misread a table. The analyst's check is fast (the source is public and specific) and covers the highest-risk element of the output — the figures clients will act on. [2][3]

## 8. Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "If I asked the AI the same question twice and got the same answer, that verifies it."**

This is false. Asking the same AI the same question twice gives you two outputs from the same model, shaped by the same training data. Both outputs can be wrong in the same way. Consistency is not the same as correctness. Verification requires an independent source — not a repetition of the original source. [2]

**Misconception 2: "I cannot verify AI output because I am not an expert."**

This is sometimes false and sometimes an overstatement. You do not always need to be an expert to verify. Cross-referencing a claim against an authoritative published standard (a law, a regulation, a reference table) does not require expertise — it requires knowing where to look and reading carefully. Domain expertise is one verification method, but it is not the only one. [3]

**Misconception 3: "If I cannot produce the same output myself, I cannot verify it."**

False. Verification and production are different things (see Section 5.2). You can verify a claim without being able to produce it independently. A non-chemist can verify that a listed chemical compound's molecular weight matches the value in a published chemistry database. They are checking a specific claim against an authoritative source, not reproducing the chemistry. [1]

## 9. Connections

### Backward links

**Q1 (9.7 — cost-of-error triage):** Q2 is always interpreted in the context of Q1. When Q1 tells you the cost of being wrong is low, a partial or difficult verification may be acceptable. When Q1 tells you the cost is high, Q2 becomes a gate — you should not act without passing it. The severity of error, probability, and detectability dimensions from 9.7 directly set the stakes for Q2.

**Automation bias (9.5):** Automation bias is the tendency to trust automated systems over human judgment — even when the automated output is wrong. Q2 is a direct counter-measure. By requiring independent verification, Q2 forces you to break the automation bias loop. You are not allowed to take the AI output at face value; you must do an additional check.

**Anchoring bias (9.4):** When a second human is the verification method, the second reviewer must not read the AI's answer first. If they do, they may be anchored to the AI's framing and fail to notice errors. This is why asking a second person works best when the reviewer forms their own view before reading the AI output.

**Confirmation bias (9.3):** Cross-referencing can be undermined if you selectively read your reference source to confirm what the AI already told you, ignoring contradictory evidence. Q2 only works if the verification is honest — you must look for disconfirming evidence, not just confirming evidence.

## 13. Facilitator Notes

**Anchoring the session with a concrete failure case.** Students respond better to Q2 when they see a real example where not verifying caused a problem — not a hypothetical. The legal domain is good for this because AI-generated case citations that do not exist are a documented failure mode. Use the prescription example: a patient does not need to be a pharmacist to check that the dosage on a label matches the reference range in a printed guide. Walk through this concrete case before moving to the table in Section 5.7.
